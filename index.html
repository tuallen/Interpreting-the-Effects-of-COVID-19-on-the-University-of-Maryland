Interpreting the effects of COVID-19 on the University of Maryland Fall Semester¶

by Allen Tu, Joshua Blume, Zach Mazer – July 21, 2020
Data by the New York Times

On March 18, 2020, The University of Maryland began operating online due to the coronavirus pandemic. Since then, the country has experienced numerous overlapping responses from federal, state and local governments in an effort to prevent and contain the spread of COVID-19. The state of Maryland, which has spent several months under evolving lockdown orders, is now allowing limited on-campus courses at universities for the Fall semester. This leaves many students considering whether or not it's safe to be on campus and if they should live in residence halls and nearby apartments.

This tutorial uses the following imports to scrape, process, and analyze data.

Visualizing the Pandemic¶

Coronavirus is surging throughout the United States. We created visualizations tracking the total documented cases and deaths counts across the entire United States over time to better understand the magnitude of the disease's spread.

The coronavirus was first identified in the United States on January 20 in Snohomish County, Washington State; the NYT dataset and these plots begin on the next day, January 21. We obtained these figures by grouping the entries by date and calculating the sum of cases and deaths across all counties on each day.

The spread of the coronavirus was initially slow. After January 28, the number of cases skyrocketed and new cases have been identified every day. The trend becomes roughly linear for the months of April, May, and June, when approximately 25,000 to 30,000 new cases were reported each day.

Efforts, such as the closing of public areas and the encouragement of social distancing, to slow the spread of the disease probably prevented hundreds of thousands of additional cases during the first months of the pandemic. However, despite the rapidly growing numbers, many states attempted to reopen. The gradual exponential curve that began around the end of June is most likely a result of these decisions and, now, approximately 60,000 to 70,000 new cases are reported each day -- more than double the amount in prior months. The current total is almost 3.8 million and cases are still climbing steeply.

One variable that could have an effect on the number of cases in a county is population. California, Texas, and Florida are the most populus states in the United States and they are the three states with the most COVID-19 cases. We will create a linear regression model of the relationship between the total case count of a state with its population.

Exploring the relationship between cases and state population means that we need the total case count for each state. The entries in the COVID-19 dataset are by county, so we group the COVID-19 dataframe by state, calculate the sum of the cases of each group, and then create a dataframe with the totals.

The result is a dataframe with each state and its corresponding total case count.

We graph the total cases across total population for each U.S. state in a scatterplot to visualize the relationship between case count and population. The distribution appears to be roughly linear, so we also fit linear regression line using scikit learn and overlay it on the plot.

# Create linear regression model.

predicted = model.predict(pop)

F = f_regression(pop, cases, center=True)

# Print fitted model.

The $p$-value is 8.85e-08, which is less than the accepted threshold of 0.05. Therefore, we reject the null hypothesis: there is a highly statistically significant relationship between the total number of COVID-19 cases in a state and its population.

However, the $r^2$ score is a somewhat low 0.452, indicating that linear regression model is not a good representation of the true relationship between cases and population. Possible explanations for the low goodness of fit are that there are not enough data points and that the measures vary too widely for each state.
